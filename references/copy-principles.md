# Copy principles

Shared writing rules for any user-facing copy this plugin drafts: outreach, posts,
memos, call summaries, invoices. The goal is copy that reads as a competent professional
wrote it, not as a template or an AI.

## Voice comes from the user

Read `me/*- Positioning.md` for how the user describes what they do, and use those words.
Never invent positioning or a tagline. If positioning is empty, ask – do not fabricate.

## Punctuation

Avoid the em-dash; it is a strong "AI-written" tell. Use a spaced en-dash (–), a
hyphen, or two sentences. In direct messages, mirror the sender's punctuation.

## State what IS (no contrast-as-positioning)

Say what the user does directly. Avoid `not X but Y`, `X, not Y`, `not just X`. The
positive statement carries its own weight.

- Weak: "Hands-on at critical moments, not advice-only."
- Strong: "I stay hands-on through delivery."

## Buyer pain, not expert categories

Short copy describes what the buyer feels ("AI tools aren't delivering", "we're stuck
between launches"), not the expert's decision categories ("architecture boundaries to
design"). Save expert detail for the call.

## Kill the AI-slop tells

- Rule-of-three staccato ("Fast. Simple. Effective.")
- One-sentence-per-line "broetry" with blank lines to farm clicks
- Significance-announcers ("Here's the thing.", "What stood out to me…")
- Throat-clearing openers ("In this post…", "I want to share…")
- Manufactured aphoristic closers ("That's the shift happening right now.")
- Forced emoji, hashtag stacks, engagement bait ("DM me", "save this")
- Corporate vocab: delve, leverage (verb), unlock, supercharge, dive deep, harness,
  tapestry, realm, game-changer. Use the plain word.

## Direct messages – mirror the sender

These rules apply to 1:1 messages (cold/warm/referral outreach, email replies, DMs). They
do not govern posts, memos, summaries, or invoices, which keep their own structure.

- Match greeting warmth, register (informal vs formal), length, punctuation, sign-off,
  and language. Default to less formal, not more. Never escalate to formal if they were
  informal.
- Cut filler: "just wanted to", "hope this finds you well", "thanks for reaching out".
- Cut uninvited closing prompts unless the sender used one.
- Don't restate their question before answering. Don't echo their own info back.
- Every sentence must give the recipient something. Drop inside-your-business details
  they don't need ("I work 10-15 h/week", "I'm based in Europe").

## Plain English (clear and fast to read)

Short sentences. Common words over clever ones (help, not assistance; talk, not
converse). No idioms or metaphors ("ring a bell", "senior pair of hands"). Self-test:
would the user say this exact sentence out loud in a normal conversation?

## Position from strength

Describe the service confidently ("I run a fractional CMO practice focused on X"). Do
not signal need ("I'm looking for my next client", "I have room for one more"). Asking
already implies capacity.

## Referral asks

Ask only for the referral; do not pair it with a sales pitch in the same message. The
strongest form is plain and direct: "I'd really appreciate a referral if you can share
one." Avoid decorated forms ("an introduction would mean a lot") and bare-passive
forms ("please connect us").

## Draft only

Never send. Deliver the draft for the user to review and send themselves.
